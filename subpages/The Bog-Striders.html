The Bog-Striders

TagsBog-striders
TextThe Bog-Striders are reptilian humanoids who remember their arrival during "The Great Drowning," which shapes their identity and survival instincts. They are tall, lean, and camouflaged with scales that vary by tribe. Their culture is pragmatic, focusing on survival and animistic spirituality, with shamans as respected leaders. Major tribes include the Moss-Skin Tribe, known for diplomacy; the aggressive Black-Scale Hunters; the stealthy Reed-Stalker Tribe; and the reclusive Deep-Dwellers. The Silt-Guards maintain peace among tribes, while the Mire-Touched are outcasts who deal with malevolent hags.

The Bog-Striders are unique among the peoples of Geoctum because they, more than any other known race, remember the day of their arrival. Their oral traditions, passed down through generations of shamans, speak of it as "The Great Drowning".

They remember a different world, a lush, primordial jungle-swamp under a warm yellow sun, teeming with vibrant life. In their history, the sky tore open without warning, the world was flooded with the "colours of chaos" (the bruised purples and blues of the Everstorm), and their entire world, its land, its creatures, its spirits, was violently pulled apart and scattered into the void. They see themselves not as settlers in a new land, but as the sole, traumatized survivors of a planetary apocalypse.

This memory is the absolute core of their identity. It instills in them a deep-seated fatalism, but also a fierce, unyielding will to survive. Having already lived through the end of one world, they are grimly determined not to lose another.

The Bog-Striders are tall, reptilian humanoids with a wiry, muscular build. They are not bulky or brutish; their bodies are lean and powerful, built for endurance and sudden bursts of predatory speed needed to hunt in the treacherous marsh. They typically stand taller than a human, with a slight forward hunch to their posture. Their scales are their most defining feature, providing excellent camouflage for their environment. The colouration varies between tribes and individuals:

  • Common colours: Most Bog-Striders have scales in shades of muddy brown, deep mossy green, and murky grey, allowing them to blend seamlessly with the stagnant water and vegetation.
  • Tribe Variation: The Black-Scale Hunters have scales with a darker, almost obsidian sheen, while the Moss-Skin Tribe often cultivates a symbiotic, shaggy moss that grows in the cracks between their scales, making them nearly invisible when still.
  • Texture: Their scales are typically small, tough, and non-reflective, unlike the more vibrant scales of other reptilian races.

They have distinctly crocodilian heads with long, powerful snouts filled with sharp, conical teeth, perfect for snatching fish and tearing meat. Their eyes are large and reptilian with slitted pupils, adapted for seeing in the murky water and the gloom of the marsh fog. Eye colours are often striking, ranging from a piercing gold or bright, venomous orange to a deep swampy green. A nictitating membrane, a clear third eyelid, protects their eyes and allows them to see clearly underwater.

Their arms and legs are long and sinewy. Their hands have three or four long fingers tipped with sharp, black claws, and their feet are partially webbed, giving them a significant advantage when swimming. They possess a long, powerful tail that acts as a rudder in the water and a crucial tool for balance when navigating fallen logs or slippery ground. In combat, a swing from their tail can easily knock an opponent off their feet.

Bog-Striders do not wear much in the way of traditional clothing. Instead, they adorn themselves with trophies from their environment that signify status and skill. Common adornments include:

  • Necklaces made from the vertebrae of giant pike or the fangs of crocodiles.
  • Bracers woven from tough reeds and hardened monster hide.
  • Polished, colourful stones retrieved from the deepest parts of the marsh.
  • The iridescent carapaces of giant marsh insects.

Culture and Society

Bog-Strider culture is deeply pragmatic. Every tool has a purpose, every action is weighed for its survival value. They are patient hunters, skilled trappers, and masters of camouflage and guerrilla warfare within their marshy domain.

Having lost their ancestral gods in the Sundering Sky, their spirituality is intensely animistic. They revere the spirits of the Great Marsh; the ancient crocodiles, the whispering reeds, the fog itself, because these things are tangible and present. Their shamans are the most respected members of the tribes, acting as spiritual guides, healers, and, most importantly, the keepers of the memory of the Great Drowning. They interpret omens to ensure the sky does not sunder again.

The Bog-Striders are not a unified nation but a collection of distinct, tribal communities bound by a shared culture and history. Their society is still developing, relying on ancient customs and traditions. Major tribes include the patient Moss-Skin Tribe, the aggressive Black-Scale Hunters, and the reclusive Reed-Stalker Tribe.

The semi-permanent encampment of Silt-Throne is not a capital city with a single ruler. It is a neutral meeting ground, a place for inter-tribal councils, seasonal trade, and major religious ceremonies. The "throne" for which it is named is a large, ceremonial seat carved from the fossilized bones of an ancient beast, used by the eldest shaman or an elected speaker during their gatherings.

Relations

The Bog-Striders are deeply territorial and xenophobic. They view all outsiders with suspicion, believing they might unknowingly bring about another cataclysm or foolishly disrespect the spirits of the marsh.

The Mire-Sisters

Their relationship with the hags of the Great Marsh is particularly complex and tense. The Mire-Sisters also remember the fall of their old world, but they treat it with cynical, mocking contempt. They see the Bog-Striders' reverence for their past as a weakness to be exploited, and they often attempt to twist their memories or offer forbidden knowledge about the event for their own cruel amusement. This fundamental clash in how they process their shared trauma makes them eternal, wary antagonists.

Tribes

The Moss-Skin Tribe

The Moss-Skins are the spiritual leaders and diplomats of the Bog-Strider people. They are the primary keepers of the oral tradition, and their shamans are considered the most powerful and wise. They believe that survival comes from understanding the Great Marsh, not just enduring it. They preach patience, observation, and harmony with the spirits of the swamp. They often act as mediators in disputes between the other tribes at the gatherings on Silt-Throne.

They are respected by all other tribes, even if the more aggressive ones see their patience as a weakness. They hold the deepest and most profound distrust for the Mire-Sisters, viewing the hags' magic as a corruption of the natural spiritualism they practice.

Members of this tribe are recognisable by the symbiotic, shaggy moss that grows in the crevices of their muted green and brown scales, providing them with extraordinary natural camouflage. This moss is a mark of pride and wisdom, with the eldest members being almost completely covered. They are not the strongest warriors but are unparalleled herbalists, alchemists, and healers. They are masters of trapping and fishing rather than aggressive hunting.

The Black-Scale Hunters

The Black-Scales are the warrior arm of the Bog-Strider race. They believe that survival is guaranteed by strength and martial dominance. They are aggressive, territorial, and expansionist, always seeking to secure more territory and resources for their people. They see the other creatures of the marsh not as spirits to be respected, but as rivals to be defeated or food to be hunted. Their philosophy is simple: the strong eat, and the weak are eaten.

The Black-Scales are typically larger and more powerfully built than other Bog-Striders. Their scales are a dark, glossy, almost obsidian black, making them intimidating figures. They favour adornments of bone, teeth, and claws from the most dangerous beasts they have slain. They are masters of open combat, wielding heavy, bone-clubs and crocodile-hide shields, and they are the primary hunters of the marsh's most dangerous megafauna, like hydras and giant crocodiles.

They have a pragmatic respect for the Moss-Skins' wisdom but often grow impatient with their cautious approach. They view the Reed-Stalkers' stealth tactics as cowardly but value their poisons for tipping the scales in a tough fight. They are openly hostile to any and all outsiders.

The Reed-Stalker Tribe

If the Black-Scales are the army, the Reed-Stalkers are the assassins and scouts. They believe that to survive is to be unseen and unheard. They operate on the fringes, viewing open conflict as a foolish waste of energy. Why meet a foe head-on when a single, well-placed poisoned dart from the shadows will suffice? They are pragmatic to an extreme, valuing efficiency and silence above honour or glory.

They are intensely secretive, even with the other tribes. They interact primarily through trade at Silt-Throne, where their poisons and intelligence are highly valued. They rarely speak in councils, preferring to listen. They avoid the Mire-Sisters at all costs, as they know the hags' magic could easily unravel the secrecy that is their greatest defence.

Reed-Stalkers are the most slender and gracile of the tribes. Their scales have a lighter base with vertical stripes of tan and dull green, allowing them to disappear completely within a stand of reeds. They move with an unnerving silence and fluidity. They are masters of stealth, ambush tactics, and toxicology, brewing the most potent and fast-acting venoms in the entire marsh.

The Silt-Guards

The Silt-Guards are not a tribe in the traditional bloodline sense, but a sworn brotherhood of warriors drawn from all other tribes. Their sole purpose is to protect the neutrality and sanctity of the meeting ground, Silt-Throne. When a Bog-Strider joins the Silt-Guards, they forsake their birth-tribe's allegiance and pledge their life to the security of all. Their philosophy is one of unwavering duty, impartiality, and the preservation of the fragile peace between the tribes.

A Silt-Guard is easily identified by their unique ceremonial armour, crafted from the polished, fossilized bones of ancient marsh leviathans. They carry shields painted with the symbol of Silt-Throne,. They are experts in defensive combat, crowd control, and de-escalation, trained to end fights without bloodshed whenever possible.

They are held in universal respect by all tribes, and their authority on Silt-Throne is absolute. To attack a Silt-Guard is to declare war on all Bog-Striders. They hold no feuds and favour no tribe during councils.

The Mire-Touched

This is a tribe of outcasts and heretics. The Mire-Touched are Bog-Striders who, through desperation, ambition, or madness, have rejected the traditional spirits and chosen to bargain with the Mire-Sisters. They believe tangible power, offered by the hags, is the only true path to survival and eventual dominance. They actively serve the hags' interests in exchange for dark gifts and forbidden magic.

They are universally despised and feared. The major tribes view them as traitors who have betrayed the memory of "The Great Drowning." Any Bog-Strider found dealing too closely with the hags risks being exiled to join the Mire-Touched. They are a constant source of internal strife, sometimes kidnapping individuals to fulfil the hags' gruesome demands.

Their association with the hags has physically and mentally warped them. Their scales are often unnaturally pale or discoloured, sometimes covered in weeping sores or magical warts. They are gaunt, with a wild, haunted look in their eyes. They are not skilled warriors but are adept at hedge magic, curses, and brewing the foul, corrupting potions taught to them by their patrons.

The Deep-Dwellers

The most reclusive and mysterious of all the tribes, the Deep-Dwellers have almost entirely forsaken the land. They believe the only true safety is in the silent, dark depths of the marsh's largest and deepest pools. Their philosophy is one of absolute isolation. They have little interest in the politics of the "surface" tribes, caring only for the goings-on in their subterranean aquatic world.

Over generations, they have developed more pronounced aquatic adaptations. They have larger, fully-webbed hands and feet, smoother skin with less prominent scales for hydrodynamic movement, and functioning gills on their necks that allow them to remain submerged indefinitely. Their eyes are larger and darker to see in the low light. They are peerless swimmers and masters of underwater combat with nets and tridents.

To the other tribes, the Deep-Dwellers are almost a myth. They rarely attend the gatherings at Silt-Throne, and their appearance is a momentous occasion, usually to deliver warnings of threats rising from the deep waters (such as a hydra growing too large or a new predator appearing). They are seen as alien and strange, but their warnings are always taken with the utmost seriousness.
