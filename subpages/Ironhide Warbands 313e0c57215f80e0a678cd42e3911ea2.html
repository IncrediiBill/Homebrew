Ironhide Warbands

TagsHalf-OrcOrcs
TextThe Ironhide Warbands are a disciplined, religiously zealous society of orcs, led by Warlord Shamans who interpret divine visions. They engage in brutal rituals and seek constant conflict, establishing fortified camps in rugged territories. Their spiritual beliefs emphasize reincarnation and proving worth through battle, guided by six War-Gods representing various aspects of warfare. The Ironhide Code, a universal set of principles, dictates their actions and defines their identity, enforcing honor and the pursuit of worth through combat. Various warbands, such as the Bone-Crown Chaplains and Ironbound Mercenaries, embody different aspects of their culture and beliefs, each with unique practices and appearances

The Ironhide Warbands are not merely savage barbarians, but a disciplined, religiously zealous warrior society, forged by constant warfare. Their royalty isn't about inherited bloodlines in the human sense, but about spiritual authority and martial prowess forged in battle. A Warlord Shaman or Battle-Seer King is the orc who possesses the most grievous, ritualistic scars (representing divine favour and past battles) and who can best interpret the fragmented visions of their war-gods or guide them to new, worthy conflicts. They carry sacred, blood-stained totems or the bones of their greatest fallen warriors from their original world, believing these imbue them with power. Their rituals involve brutal gladiatorial contests, ritual scarring, and fervent prayers for guidance to their distant, fractured deities.

They gravitate towards desolate, rugged frontiers or volcanic badlands (like the Ash Wastes, clashing with Dragonborn Clans). They also try to carve out territories in rough, untamed plains bordering more settled regions, seeking constant conflict. Unlike the nomadic Wilders, the Ironhide Warbands try to establish heavily fortified, if crude, camps and strongholds, reflecting their ingrained military discipline. They are a constant, violent threat to outlying Mehdian settlements, Mixed-Race Towns, and sometimes the less fortified borders of Vanalir lands, seeing them as opportunities for plunder and worthy foes.

The War‑Gods

The War-Gods of the Orcs are split into 3 pairs, each represents a factor of war. The First Pair represent the push and pull of battle. The Second Pair represent the overwhelming force vs the subtle kill. The Final Pair are the end of battle and the reason it must begin again.

Grimu, The God of Fury (Offense)

The embodiment of the unstoppable charge, berserk strength, and the will to break enemies. Worshipped by shock troops and duellists. Rituals involve bloodletting, roaring chants, and frenzied combat. Scars from this god are jagged, chaotic, often self‑inflicted.

Sana, The Goddess of Endurance (Defence)

The patient shield, the unbroken line, the stubborn refusal to fall. Favoured by shield‑bearers, sentinels, and warband guardians. Rituals involve fasting, pain trials, and long vigils. Scars are straight, deliberate, often burned rather than cut.

Ragash, The God of Ruin (Destruction)

Represents fire, siege craft, razing, and the cleansing of the old to make way for the new. Worshipped by siege engineers, fire‑priests, and raiders. Totems often include charred bones or volcanic stone. Scars are burned in spirals or cracked patterns.

Mirket, The Goddess of the Hunt (Cunning, Pursuit and Precision)

The patron of relentless pursuit, tracking, and the kill. Favoured by scouts, hunters, beast-tamers, and skirmishers. Rituals involve silence, night vigils, and smoke‑mask rites. Scars are thin, precise, often hidden. Her omens appear in claw marks, blood trails, and animal behaviour.

Bugrol, The God of the Fallen (Death)

The keeper of the honoured dead, the judge of worthy deaths, and the whisperer of ancestral memory. Worshipped by shamans, bone‑keepers, and war‑priests. Rituals involve bone‑rattling chants, funerary pyres, and scarification over old wounds. Scars resemble rib‑patterns or skeletal motifs.

Orhan, The Goddess of Prophecy (Fate)

The fractured oracle whose visions guide the warbands toward conflict. Favoured by seers, vision‑scarred warlords, and omen‑readers. Rituals involve trance states, blood‑ink tattoos, and inhaled volcanic fumes. Scars are geometric, symmetrical, often carved during visions.

The six gods are like a dysfunctional war‑camp. Each god pushes their followers into conflict not just for mortal reasons, but to spite or weaken their divine rivals. Fury and Endurance clash over whether glory comes from reckless aggression or unbreakable discipline. Ruin and Shadows sabotage each other’s plans, one wants open devastation, the other wants subtle, precise strikes. Prophecy manipulates all sides, sending contradictory visions that spark holy wars. The Fallen quietly benefits from every death, growing stronger.

Spiritual Beliefs

Worthy orcs are reincarnated and so the orcs become obsessed with proving worthiness. Death in battle is not a tragedy but a promotion, a return to the cycle with greater strength. Cowardice is spiritual suicide, because the Fallen only reincarnates the brave. Totems made from ancestral bones may contain echoes of past lives, giving shamans access to ancestral memory. Some orcs believe they are reincarnations of legendary warriors, creating rival claims of identity.

When an orc fails these standards, their soul falls outside the cycle. The soul becomes a “Hollow Echo”, a faint, drifting remnant unable to return to flesh. Shamans describe them as whispers in the wind or flickers in firelight. The soul becomes trapped in the bones of the unworthy dead, inert, unable to guide or empower descendants.

The Ironhide Code

The Code is not just a list of rules; it is believed to be the first gift of the gods, carved into the bones of the first orcs. Every warband interprets the gods differently, but the Code is universal because it is older than the pantheon’s feuds. It defines what it means to be an orc, and breaking it is seen as a spiritual betrayal.

Three principles sit at the heart of it:

  • Strength must be proven. Worth is demonstrated through action, not birth.
  • Honour must be visible. Scars, deeds, and duels are the language of truth.
  • The gods must be obeyed. Visions, omens, and ancestral memory are sacred commands.

Because the Code is universal, it creates a shared identity across all Ironhide Warbands. Warlords rule by proving worth, not by lineage. Shamans act as judges, interpreting whether actions uphold or violate the Code. Children are raised on stories of worth, learning early that every action is a test. Warbands rarely unite, because each believes it must prove its own worth through conflict. Diplomacy is rare, but when it happens, it is framed as a duel of words or wills. The Code also explains why the Ironhides are feared: they are predictable in their honour, but relentless in their pursuit of worth.

Core Tenets

The Law of the Unbroken Line

An orc must never retreat unless commanded by a warlord‑shaman who has received a divine sign. Retreat without cause is seen as spiritual collapse. Those who flee are believed to “break the line” in the afterlife as well, losing their place among the ancestors.

The Law of the Open Scars

Scars are sacred records of worth. Covering them is considered deceit. Altering or disguising them is a grave offence. Shamans read scars like scripture; hiding them is like hiding sins.

The Law of the Worthy Duel

Challenges must be answered. Refusing a duel is refusing to let the gods judge you. Even warlords must accept duels unless a vision forbids it. Duels are not always to the death, some are tests of endurance, strength, or will.

The Law of the True Vision

Visions, omens, and prophetic signs must be obeyed. Ignoring a vision is ignoring the gods. False visions (claimed without proof) are punished harshly, as they mislead the warband. Shamans who misinterpret visions may be scarred ritually to correct their sight.

The Law of the Honoured Dead

The dead must be treated according to their worth. Worthy orcs are given to the God of the Fallen through ritual pyres or bone‑keeping rites. Unworthy orcs are denied these rites, their bones left bare or broken. Desecrating a worthy corpse is one of the greatest taboos.

The Law of the Unending Struggle

Conflict is sacred. Peace is tolerated only as preparation for the next worthy battle. Warbands must seek challenges, not comfort. Cowardice is not just a personal failing, it weakens the entire cycle of reincarnation.

Consequences for Breaking the Code

Breaking the Code does not always mean death, but it always means dishonour.

  • Names may be stripped away.
  • Scars may be ritually marred or overwritten.
  • Totems may reject the offender’s bones.
  • The warband may exile them as “Hollow Walkers”, living reminders of failure.
  • The God of the Fallen will not reincarnate them.

The Warbands

The Bone‑Crown Chaplains

The Chaplains are the spiritual heart of all Ironhide Warbands. They are not a warband in the traditional sense, they are a neutral priesthood that every warband relies on for:

  • Death rites (Death‑Readers)
  • Reincarnation rituals (Bone‑Keepers)
  • Mediation of disputes (Worth‑Judges)

Their authority comes from the God of the Fallen, and every orc believes that only the Chaplains can ensure their soul returns worthy.

The Chaplains believe they are the direct descendants of the original orcs from the lost world. Their doctrine claims:

  • Their bloodline carries the oldest scars
  • Their bones hold the clearest ancestral echoes
  • Their rites are unchanged since the First War
  • Other warbands are “branches,” but they are the “root”

Every warband hosts a small group of Chaplains. These detachments are respected but kept at arm’s length. They are not friends. They are not comrades. They are the eyes of Bugrol.

Warbands pay the Chaplains in:

  • Captured relics
  • Oaths of service
  • Rare materials (obsidian, volcanic glass, frost-iron, etc.)

Refusing to pay is unthinkable. A warband that denies the Chaplains their due risks:

  • Their dead being declared unworthy
  • Their ancestors refusing reincarnation
  • Their totems losing spiritual power
  • Other warbands treating them as cursed

The Chaplains don’t need to threaten anyone, the Ironhide Code does it for them.

The Orcs of the Bone-Crown Chaplains have coarse black hair, tall and muscular, with tough, leathery skin. Their skin tones are shades of grey, and those born with the palest skin colours are destined to do great things for the Warband. They wear armour made from metal and bones. Their helmets feature a skull for the faceplate and jagged bone pieces in the form of a crown. Their chest armour features a ribcage. Their staves are lined with bones of honoured dead.

The Warband’s primary Settlement is located in the Northern Wildfang Thicket.

The Ironbound Mercenaries

To outsiders, they are terrifying but reliable. To other orcs, they are controversial but undeniably worthy. The Ironbound Mercenaries are a collection of Orcs who sell their skills for gold. They see themselves not as sellswords, but as contracted challengers, warriors lending their strength to shape conflict.

They fight for outsiders if doing so:

  • Proves their worth
  • Fulfils a divine vision
  • Offers worthy enemies
  • Allows them to test themselves in unfamiliar lands
  • Lets them influence foreign wars in their gods’ favour

They follow Orhan believing each conflict to be part of their larger destiny. They believe that the gods want them to spread conflict across the world and these foreign wars offer new tests of worth.

They are seen as an extremely reliable group to anyone that needs muscle for a job. They never break a contract, that would be dishonour. They refuse jobs involving cowardly acts (massacres, ambushes of the weak, etc.). They will turn on an employer who violates the Ironhide Code.

The Orcs of the Ironbound Mercenaries are more human in appearance, and the only warband that allows Half-orcs to join. They are still tall and muscular, with small tusks appearing from their mouths, but lack the broad stopped builds of most orcs. They use tokens from their contracts to decorate their appearance. Their scars are marked with foreign symbols representing battles abroad. Totems are decorated with trophies from distant lands and their armour is adapted from multiple cultures, reforged in Ironhide style.

Anyone wishing to petition the Warband must either travel to their settlement in the south of the Heartland, bordering the Wildfang Thicket, or be lucky (or unlucky) enough to come across one of their mercenaries in an inn or on the road.

The Frost‑Bound

The Frost‑Bound are a collection of smaller warbands who believe the Frost‑Heart Tundra, Icedrift Peaks, and Nehzin’s Glacier were shaped by Sana, the Goddess of Endurance, as a living crucible. Every blizzard, crevasse, and white‑out is a divine test. They believe the Frost‑Heart Dragonborn were placed in the tundra by the goddess as rivals, not enemies, and that their war is a sacred contest, a proving ground for both peoples.

Every aspect of their society is built around surviving the unforgiving north.

  • Children are exposed to the cold early to “meet the goddess.”
  • Warriors fast before battle to prove discipline.
  • Scars are frostbitten deliberately, creating pale, deadened patterns.
  • Their armour is layered hide, bone, and glacier‑forged metal.

Before major battles, Frost‑Bound warriors partake in the Cold Vigil. They stand motionless in the snow for hours or days, letting the cold seep into their bones. Only those who remain standing are deemed worthy to fight.

When a Frost‑Bound warrior swears an oath, they carve it into ice and leave it exposed. If the ice survives the night without cracking, the oath is accepted by the goddess.

Each Warband is controlled by an Ice‑Marked Warlord, a warrior whose body bears the most frostbite scars without losing limbs. Their Shamans are the Frost-Seers, who interpret the patterns of cracking ice, drifting snow, and auroras.

The Orcs of the Frost-Bound have broad, muscular builds with thick necks and heavy shoulders. Their skin tones ranging from deep grey to pale frost‑green. They feature prominent tusks, often capped with bone or metal. Their hair is long and braided or with topknots, sometimes frozen stiff in winter. Instead of heavy armour, they wear layered furs from tundra beasts with heavy cloaks made from white wolf pelts. Their armour is made of leather with bones to reinforce their strength. They wear the heads of beasts as headgear

The Ash‑Scar Legion

The Ash‑Scar Legion believes the Grimu, God of Ruin, forged them in flame. Every eruption, every plume of ash, every tremor is a divine command. They scar themselves with burning pumice, branding their skin with cracked, ember‑like patterns. The Ash‑Scar Legion is surrounded by foes who embody different aspects of fire and shadow. They see the Red Dragon as divine beast to be slain or tamed. Each conflict shapes the Legion’s culture.

To them:

  • Pain is proof of devotion
  • Fire reveals the worthy
  • Ash is sacred dust from the gods’ forge

They see the volcanic badlands as holy ground.

The Legion is controlled by a Pyrelord, chosen through trials of endurance in ash storms and volcanic heat. Each front the Legion fight on is controlled by an Ash‑Marshal, a warrior chosen by the the Warlord to coordinate the many camps in the Ash-Wastes and oversee the discipline in the ranks of the Warbands.

The Ash-Scar orcs have ash‑grey or charcoal‑black skin with ember‑red eyes. Their scars form cracked patterns on their skin, with an occasional faint glow appearing out them in the night. Their armour is as dark as their surroundings, made of Obsidian plates. Their weapons are often imbued glowing orange runes that cause them to ignite when they are in combat. The Ash‑Seer is their spiritual leader who interprets the smoke of Mount Cinder, and they lead the Ember-Shamans who bless their warriors and apply flame runes to their weapons.

The Blood-Trail Stalkers

The Blood‑Trail Stalkers believe the Goddess of the Hunt created all prey so that orcs could prove their worth through pursuit. To them, the hunt is not sport, it is a sacred trial of endurance, instinct, and honour. They do not ambush the weak. They do not kill from afar. They chase, corner, and finish their prey face‑to‑face.

They are found in dense forests, tangled roots, and beast‑haunted glades make this the perfect domain for a warband that thrives on tracking and pursuit. Their camps move with the herds and the seasons.

The Blood-Trail stalkers culture is built around the hunt:

  • They teach young orcs that death in the hunt is sacred
  • They reinforce the idea that the goddess rewards worthy prey
  • They create heroes whose deeds echo for generations
  • They give the warband a constant source of purpose and challenge

The First Scent
Young orcs are blindfolded and led into the forest. They must track a marked beast using only smell and instinct. Success earns their first scar.

The Last Breath
A warrior must look their prey in the eyes as it dies. Turning away is dishonour.

The Red Feast
After a great hunt, the warband shares the heart of the quarry. This binds them as one pack.

The Hunt‑Oath
Oaths are sworn over fresh blood. Breaking one is a spiritual death.

The Blood-Trail Stalkers mark creatures as legendary. A creature becomes legendary when it kills 5 Stalkers who have hunted it. Not by accident. Not by surprise. But in a true hunt, a contest of endurance, instinct, and will. Once this happens it is marked as Mirket-Chosen, it’s name is carved into the warband’s Hunt-Stone, and it’s territory becomes sacred ground. To the Stalkers, these beasts are not monsters, they are rivals, elevated by the goddess herself.

The camps are controlled by Trail-Mothers or Trail-Fathers who are elders that teach tracking, scent-reading and beast lore. They are revered as the living memory of the hunt. The hunters of the clans, the Blood-Markers, are trained by Shamans to cast bloodrunes on prey, ensuring the warband can track it for days. The Warbands also have Beast-Bonded Orcs who raise and bond with predators, their bond is lifelong, if one dies, the other enters mourning. The most elite hunters of the warbands are Fangs, warriors who are able to hunt and challenge any beast.

They are massive green orcs who wear the furs and leathers of the creatures they have killed. They wear the bones, teeth, and fangs of the creatures they kill on hunts as trophies.

The Ruin‑Tide Reavers

The Ruin‑Tide Reavers worship entropy itself, the slow, inevitable collapse of all things. They believe the Ragash is not just flame and eruption, but decay, erosion, collapse, the breaking of cycles, and the end of all structures. They see themselves as the god’s chosen instruments to unmake what others build.

They have no homeland. Their domain is:

  • the open ocean
  • the storm‑wracked coasts
  • The Void
  • the fog‑choked waters near The Void
  • the shattered reefs where ships go to die

Their ships are floating fortresses of bone, iron, and driftwood, held together by ritual bindings and sheer stubbornness.

Reavers look like they’ve been carved by salt and storm. Their skin tones are sea‑grey or storm‑blue. Their hair is salt‑bleached. They wear dark armour covered in barnacles, scars, and chains wrapped around limbs. They have tattoos of waves, spirals, and void‑runes. Their scars resemble cracks in ship hulls.

Each ships is ruled by a Tide‑Prophet, shamans who read omens in tides, storms, and drowned corpses. They claim to hear the God of Ruin in the crash of waves. They have Storm‑Callers who are drummers and chanters that use rhythm to steady the crew during storms
