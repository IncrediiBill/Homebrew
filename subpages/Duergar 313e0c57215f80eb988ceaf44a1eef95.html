The Duergar are the grim, avaricious Grey Dwarves of Geoctum's Beneath. While they share ancestral ties with The Stoneheart Empire, their long isolation and exposure to the alien depths have twisted them into a ruthless, xenophobic, and entirely self-serving society. Their lives revolve around relentless mining, brutal craftsmanship, and the expansion of their oppressive domain, largely fuelled by the relentless exploitation of slave labour.

Current Political Climate & Relations

Duergar politics are a brutal meritocracy of power and wealth, driven by internal ambition and external aggression. Duergar cities are ruled by a Tyrant-King/Queen or a council of powerful Thane-Lords, whose authority is maintained through a combination of fear, military might, and control over resources (especially slave populations). Long generations underground have made them sensitive to natural sunlight, which they find painful and disorienting.

Their entire economy and social structure are built upon slavery. Slave pens are as common as smithies, and a Duergar's wealth is often measured in the number of thralls they command. Despite harsh measures, slave rebellions (particularly by desperate Svirfneblin or captured surface humanoids) are a persistent threat, requiring constant vigilance and brutal suppression. Their reliance on slavery and brutal methods often stifles true innovation. While efficient at what they do, they rarely develop truly new ideas without stealing them.

Duergar craftsmanship is renowned for its strength, durability, and raw power, but it lacks the artistry of The Stoneheart Empire. Their focus is on brutal efficiency: weapons that cleave, armour that deflects, and mines that yield. Their aesthetics reflect their grim nature. Architecture is typically blocky, utilitarian, and built for defence, using dark, unpolished stone and cold, grey steel.

The constant ambition among Thane-Lords and rising commanders leads to frequent backstabbing, assassinations, and minor conflicts that can destabilize cities. Loyalty is bought through shares of plunder or maintained through constant displays of strength. Treachery is common, and internal power struggles are a constant undercurrent.

Relationship with other Nations

Surface Nations

The Duergar have nothing but scorn for surface dwellers, viewing them as soft, weak, and oblivious. They believe the surface world's resources are theirs for the taking, and its inhabitants are merely potential slaves. They view any strange phenomena as natural oddities or opportunities for exploitation.

The Stoneheart Empire

There is a deep, abiding hatred between the Duergar and The Stoneheart Empire Dwarves. The Duergar view The Stoneheart Empire as weak, soft, and foolish for not embracing the efficiency of slavery and for clinging to surface concerns. Their shared ancestry only makes the contempt stronger. Skirmishes along deep mining borders are constant, with the Duergar typically trying to push into The Stoneheart Empire's territory for resources or slaves.

Despite their hatred, the Duergar are pragmatic. When the Drow push hard against both The Stoneheart Empire and Duergar borders, the Duergar might enter into unspoken, temporary ceasefires or even indirect, mutually beneficial tactical manoeuvrers with The Stoneheart Empire to repel the Drow. This alliance of convenience never lasts.

Svirfneblin

The Duergar view the Svirfneblin as their rightful chattel. Vast numbers of Svirfneblin are enslaved in Duergar mines and workshops, forced to labour under horrific conditions. Raids on free Svirfneblin settlements are frequent and brutal, serving as slave drives.

Drow

The Duergar are in constant, bloody conflict with the Drow for control of Underdark territory and resources. They despise the Drow's chaotic cruelty and arrogance, but they also grudgingly respect their ruthlessness and efficiency in slave management, the Duergar got the idea of slavery from the Drow. Battles between Duergar legions and Drow raiding parties are common, creating volatile zones.

Mind Flayers

Duergar fear and loathe Mind Flayers. They understand the horror of brain-eating and mental enslavement. While they usually try to avoid contact, a desperate Thane-Lord might forge a dangerous, temporary alliance with Mind Flayers to destroy a particularly strong Drow rival, or to gain arcane/psionic secrets, always hoping to betray the illithids later. They will trade slave brains for favours.

Religion

For the Duergar of Geoctum, their faith is a dark, mirror image of the Stoneheart Dwarves. Their society is built on a foundation of slavery, brutal efficiency, and a deep-seated hatred for their soft surface cousins.

The Patron: Laduguer (The Exile)

The Duergar believe Laduguer was cast out from the Dwarven pantheon (Moradin’s circle). They see themselves as his true heirs, the ones who survived the crushing depths while the surface dwarves played in the light. To the Duergar, the silence of the gods is proof that only the strong (themselves) deserve to rule. They believe Laduguer is not gone, but simply working, and they honour him through endless, joyless labour in their forges.

The Taskmistress: Deep Duerra (War/Knowledge)

While the Duergar are not naturally psionic like Mind Flayers, centuries in the Underdark have allowed some to develop stolen psionic traits like enlargement or invisibility. They credit these gifts to Duerra. She is the patron of their Inquisitors who root out dissent among slaves and Duergar alike. To a Duergar, Knowledge isn't for sharing; it's a weapon used to dominate others.

The God of the Cold Forge: Abbathor (Greed)

They do not see greed as a sin, but as the only honest motivation in a fragmented world. While the Stoneheart Dwarves might pray to Moradin for the art of smithing, the Duergar pray to the Spirit of the Mine (Abbathor) to find the richest veins of obsidian and grey steel.

The Great Puller

The Duergar are pragmatists. Like the Vanalir, they acknowledge a "Great Puller" or "Shaper of Worlds". However, while the Vanalir study this force with melancholy, the Duergar see it as a competitor. They view the shifting planar energies not as a mystery, but as a resource to be exploited or a threat to be barricaded against.
